How to solve true or false questions

True or false questions on examinations should not cause you undue worry. Simply use tactics like these and try to be the best you can be.

Understanding a few test-taking best practices is one method to help alleviate this anxiety. Here are some things to think about when it comes to true or false exam questions in particular.

1. Examine the sentence phrase by phrase and word by word.

Another useful method is to check whether the statement is accurate by looking at each word and/or phrase inside it. This is due to the fact that if any portion of the assertion is untrue, the complete statement must be wrong.

“Spaghetti sauce with tomatoes and blue color” is an example of this. While spaghetti sauce does include tomatoes, the color of the sauce is red. As a result, even if the first portion (spaghetti sauce contains tomatoes) is correct, this assertion is untrue.

2. Look for terms that are rigid.

Are they genuinely hungry 24 hours a day, seven days a week, if you hear someone state they’re always hungry? No. Instead, they are most likely implying that they are frequently hungry.

Though word choice isn’t crucial in this pleasant environment, when it comes to determining whether or not a test question is accurate, a rigid and inflexible word might quickly affect your response.

As a result, search for terms like always and never while reading true or false questions, as very few things in life happen always or never, and there are usually exceptions to every rule.

Other terms like seldom, typically, rarely, or oftentimes are more flexible and hence may be more true than untrue.

3. Carefully read the questions.

In true-or-false exam questions, it’s not unusual for a single word to determine whether a statement is true or untrue.

For example, the statement “the sky is blue” is valid, but the statement “the sky is always blue” is not always correct when overcast days or the dark, nighttime sky are taken into consideration.

That is why it is good to read these sorts of questions slowly. This ensures that you understand exactly which statement you agree or disagree with.

4. If everything else fails, make a guess.

If you’ve tried the preceding ways and are still unclear if the right answer is true or false, simply guess.

You have a 50% probability of being correct because there are only two viable responses. Plus, if the test is timed, you don’t want to get stuck on a single question for too long.

That said, saving all of the true-or-false questions you’re dubious about until the conclusion of the exam isn’t a bad idea. That way, you’ll have ample time to accurately answer the questions you do know, resulting in a better overall score.

5. Negatives should not be confused with positives.

When a true-or-false question includes a negative term like not or cannot, it might be a little perplexing.

Remove the negative word from the phrase and read it again to avoid making a mistake because of the negatives. Is this the new version? If this is the case, your negative response to the question is incorrect.

For example, if the statement is “a duck cannot swim,” you would alter it to “a duck can swim” and consider whether it is true. Because ducks can swim, the first assertion is incorrect.

When phrases contain two negatives, they might become even more perplexing. When this happens, the statement’s context is really favorable, so pay attention to it.
